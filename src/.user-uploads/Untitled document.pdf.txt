# Test 1.1 - What is the Phoneless Sales Machine?
## Result:
The Phoneless Sales Machine (PSM) is Travis's system for closing high-ticket sales — including five-figure offers — entirely through written messages. No phone calls, no webinars, no video funnels. Just text.
The engine is a three-message sequence: T1, T2, T3.
The T1 (the "tapper") goes out to an existing audience. Its only job is to identify hand-raisers — people who reply and signal interest. You're not pitching; you're sorting.
The T2 opens a real conversation with those hand-raisers. One qualifying question, something like "can you tell me a little about your situation?" The T2 is where a lot of the selling actually happens — people start telling you exactly what they want, and the sale is often mentally done before you ever send a price. Travis puts it plainly: "a lot of the sailing is really done in the T2." [1]
The T3 closes. It gives the details — the offer, the investment, what happens next. Travis recommends starting with the short T3 template to force clarity on the offer before you even write the T1. [1]
The system is also diagnostic. If T1 produces hand-raisers but T2 stalls, the T2 is the problem. If T2 converts but T3 misses, the offer or the close needs work. You fix one stage at a time. [2]
The deeper point: the market tells you what to write. The T2 conversations surface the "currency" (what people are really buying) and the "sharks" (the objections). That data writes your next T1 and sharpens your T3. You're not guessing — you're listening. [1]
Travis built this to escape calendar chaos and the overhead of managing salespeople on phones. Once a campaign is dialed in, you hand it to a tapper and it runs as an income stream while you set up the next one. [1]

[5:52](https://big-mojo.mykajabi.com/products/beamer/categories/2149957637/posts/2156382563) for the overview of how T1/T2/T3 fit together. [1]

## Test 1.2 - Walk me through the Coffee Date model.
**Result:**
Good stuff — Travis explains this one really well in his own words. Let me walk you through it.
---
The Coffee Date model is how Travis structures the entire partner relationship arc — from first contact to closed deal. He frames it as a dating progression: coffee date → first date → marriage. Each stage has one job, and you don't skip ahead.
The coffee date has exactly one purpose: to find out if a first date makes sense.
That's it. You're not pitching. You're not convincing anyone of anything. You're sizing up the opportunity and seeing if there's a reason to go deeper. Travis is explicit: "You don't try to get on a coffee date to try to marry them." [1]
Here's how he models the actual outreach. He gives this example with a partner named Ron who runs a program called Mercenaries:
&gt; "Hey Ron, I know with the move and everything you're probably all jammed up — how much room do you have? How many more mercenaries can you take on every month if you didn't have all the other stuff going on? ... Look, I don't know if I can help you or not. But would it be crazy for us to jump on, have some coffee over Zoom, and see if I can help fill that gap? I may not be able to do zero. Be able to do five or ten of those. But is that big enough that it makes sense to see if we can even explore it?" [1]
Notice what he's doing there. He's quantifying the opportunity in their terms (50 grand a month), he's positioning himself as uncertain whether he can even help, and he's making the ask feel completely low-stakes. No pitch, no deck, no "here's my system."
On the call itself, he's not convincing — he's asking. "Tell me a little about mercenaries." He wants to understand the program, where it sits in their business (main offer or side thing), what capacity looks like, what's already working. The goal is to find the found money — revenue they're leaving on the table that he can go get without touching their main bread and butter. [1]
That "found money" framing is critical. Travis wants to stay out of the partner's primary income flow. If you position yourself as helping sell their main thing, you become a vendor competing with other vendors — and you can get dropped for a cheaper option the moment you've done the work. [2] Found money is different: it's revenue they weren't going to get anyway, so there's no competition and no resentment.
If the coffee date goes well and there's a real opportunity, then you move to the first date — which is where you start talking specifics: what the campaign looks like, how the list gets used, what the split is. Marriage is when you've run a campaign together, it worked, and now you're building something ongoing.
The most common mistake Travis sees: people treat the coffee date like a marriage proposal. They show up with a full plan, try to close everything on the first call, and spook the partner. Or they go the other way — they get so excited when a partner says yes that they skip straight to execution without slowing down to actually qualify whether the opportunity is worth pursuing. [2]
One more thing worth noting: how you get to the coffee date matters. Travis says the personal opener — something genuinely specific to that person, not something you could copy-paste to a hundred people — is what separates a response from silence. "I love your podcast" isn't personal. Referencing something specific from their Facebook profile, a recent move, the wildlife in their backyard — that's personal. [3]

# Test 1.3 - What does Travis say about hand-raisers?
Result:
Here's what Travis teaches about hand-raisers, pulled together from across the library.
---
The core idea is simple but it changes how you think about selling: you don't sell to a list — you sell to the hand-raisers a list produces.
A list is just potential. Hand-raisers are the people who respond to a T1, signal interest, and self-select into the conversation. They've already done the hardest part of the sales process for you — they told you they want to talk. That's a fundamentally different person than someone you're cold-pitching.
Travis's whole PSM system is organized around this. The T1 isn't a sales message — it's a sorting mechanism. Its only job is to surface the hand-raisers. Once someone raises their hand, the close rate jumps dramatically (Travis puts it around 50% in his experience). The T2 and T3 then do their work on a much warmer, self-selected group.
Hand-raisers aren't just for direct sales either. Travis uses them as a data source. When people respond to a T1, they tell you what they're actually buying — their language, their real problem, their objections. He calls these the "currency" (what they're really after) and the "sharks" (the objections that come up). That data then writes better T1s, sharper T3s, and tighter offers. The market writes the marketing for you. [1]
One student in the 2-Page Google Doc Bonus Call described running what was essentially a hybrid T1/T2 post — he surfaced hand-raisers publicly and qualified them in the same move. Travis's reaction: "A lot of hand-raisers on the post and then a lot of people messaged me as well. That's awesome." The point being that a well-positioned offer in the right context can generate hand-raisers from multiple angles simultaneously. [2]
Travis also applies the hand-raiser logic to his own operations at scale. His description of how his business actually runs: "I'm letting marketing and the mail and the email get the hand-raisers, and then my tappers tap them in." The email or post does the sorting; the tapper handles the conversation. That's the machine. [3]
The subject line note from the 2-Page Google Doc session is worth keeping in mind too — Travis says "RSVP" as a subject line got him "lots of opens and lots of hand-raisers." One
word, no capitalization, no hype. It works because it signals a specific, low-pressure ask rather than a pitch. [2]
The practical takeaway: don't try to sell everyone on your list. Send a T1, find out who raises their hand, and then go deep with those people. The hand-raisers are the asset — everything else is just traffic.
